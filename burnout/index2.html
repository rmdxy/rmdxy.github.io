I. Problem Definition

Looking for the key needs of the user and turning these into design solutions.

Research Methodology

Keeping in mind the private and confidential nature of social work, we used a 'Concurrent Triangulation of Methods' approach consisting of interviews, questionnaires and online ethnographies to draw as much information as possible. We narrowed our research tools to these three as other methods like contextual observation could not be performed without risking the rapport and safety of both social worker and client.

Taking Care of Our Participants

To ensure that we kept our work to a professional standard, we looked at how researchers were able to extract information whilst being ethical and most importantly being able to have their subject at ease. Therefore,

  1. All of our research and test participants were asked for consent
  2. We focused on letting context-aware participants know that the interview could bring up previous experiences and potential for distressing memories
  3. We did not call social workers, as we understood the busy nature of social work
  4. Gave contacts the freedom of place, time and method to make them feel at ease
  5. We provided the option to use physical note taking as an alternative to protect the social worker from any legal concerns.

The High Priority Problems

A comparison between the main themes stemming from the primary data reveals that burnout in social work is caused by three main factors:

1. High caseload in public sector and individual client tensions

2. Management /organisation structure and attitudes

3. An overload of paperwork and irrelevant data

Affinity Diagramming

Initially, online affinity diagram tools were used to attempt and visually map these statements to cluster by apparent similarity, mimicking the physical process of writing on individual post it notes. However, due to unrealistic time constraints and the sheer number of statements, it was deemed not appropriate to use visual methods such as Miro or Trello. Subsequently, the process of affinity diagramming was initialised by going through the data, noting down different themes from each idea and colour coding similar themes. Then, through using excel spreadsheets was the data able to be sorted by similarities.

Further Findings

However, through affinity diagramming and formulating insights, it was clear that the problem was much more complex than just answering the hypothesis. There were different dimensions to burnout in social work.

For example, issues with the business model in the public sector increases caseload and hence paperwork, further increasing tension with management and supervision. The medical approach of supporting diagnostics creates tension with the business approach and privatisation of social work, as private practitioners desire different work approaches but have to comply with the medical approach, leading to support that may be less efficient at solving problems, increasing stress on the private worker due to negotiating payment and writing extra paperwork for solutions that aren’t necessarily required.

Acknowledging that we can’t solve everything

Through group synthesis, we identified that these issues were large, complex issues that required not only major organisational change, but also industry-wide reforms. The problem with the majority of these problems is that they are people problems, and the core issues surrounding social work cannot be solved by simply developing an interface.

Hence, by encouraging workers to recognise, reflect, be more proactive about their burnout/stress levels before it becomes too late, we hope to create an individualised solution that reduces burnout and to use the product as a potential stepping stone for future shifts in industry perspectives.

Defining a goal and creating a rationale for success specifically tailored to our industry.

We built our Success Criteria using the Pugh Controlled Convergence (commonly Pugh Matrix), enabling us to accurately match product with research insights.

II. Testing Approach

Closing the gap between the designer’s conceptual model and the user’s mental model of the product.

Proving Concept Validity, Utility and Usabilty

Using a blend of quantitative and qualitative approaches helps to provide a better understanding of user problems, increase validity of results and to assist in explaining findings from the qualitative data.

We used semi-structured post mortem discussions, think-aloud protocol, system usability scale and co-design workshops in our testing to help generate insights.

Selecting Participants

Context Aware

We are approaching our user evaluation partly through the lens of more experienced social workers who have been able to reflect or pickup on their burnout. Consequently, they could have conceptualised their own solutions and through this comparison to their own experience, we can reflect and iterate our solution to better fit the needs of social workers.

We want the application to be designed for social workers in their working context. Therefore, the number of usability participants should not heavily outweigh utility participants, otherwise product iterations will be geared towards users that aren’t social workers and consequently fail to address the problem scenario.

General Usabiity

The other group consists of participants that are not directly involved with the utility of the concept, but rather focus solely on the concept’s usability. The demographic of this group is more flexible than the other participant group, as context is not relevant to usability.

III. Prototyping Phase

Low Fidelity Concept Flowchart

By creating a user journey map based off one of our previous personas, we were able to focus on how the application may be integrated into our target user's work schedule. At this point, we put the concept on paper through the use of a flowchart. We approached making our concept real, using persona based walkthroughs to visualise the process of using our product.

Following the creation of our conceptual flow chart, we created rough sketches for salient screen aspects, and linked these rough sketches back to our flowchart to create a linear wireflow.

Paper Prototyping

Consequently, we felt confident and keen to create our paper prototypes for initial user testing. The use of paper prototypes allows us to identify and address issues at low costs, and avoids sticking with weak ideas for too long or ideas too complex.

Summary of Results

Through our pilot test, we found that there were a multitude of issues regarding the prototype's utility and usability. as expected. The problem was that we had thought of the entire solution mentally but did not translate the concept well enough through our first prototype. This led to us rushing through the iterating process because we felt as though we had a viable solution.

Through reflection, we found that the app’s only purpose was to track breaks, but the premise of breaks are objective and numbers based - you can have the highest amount of break-time but if this time is not efficient in recognising/reducing burnout, then what’s the point?

Creating a Mid-Fidelity Wireflow & Wireframe

New application flowcharts helped us visualise how the features of the app would interact with each other. Screens were created based on the previous flowcharts, specifically, drawing on from the features available within the app. This led us to the creation of a rough wireframe on Adobe XD.

Testing Protocol

Informed by our pilot test, accurately and reliably obtaining feedback is crucical to the development of our mid fidelity prototype. Therefore, the development of a Lo-fi Prototype testing protocol was to ensure that results were kept consistent, that it would not be skewed by asking varying questions - or that the varying results resulted from different user experiences rather than a communication error.

Summary of Results

By grouping results together, we saw a few emerging themes within our product and how different our mental model of social work was compared to our users.

For example, we made the application based on the assumption that social workers would be free to take breaks as they wish. However, conversing with our target users proved to be vital in informing changes to the app’s approach. During testing, It was stated that social workers should not reflect so much as to negatively affect their emotional wellbeing through compassion fatigue. This corroborated with our pilot test, in that users do not reflect deeply in the workplace and that “The app requires too much reflection about breaks at work.”

Updating to a High-Fidelity Wireflow & Wireframe

Using Figma, we were able to collaborate and create wireframes based off our feedback/suggestions. One person lead the overall design aesthetic, so that the design was more consistent.

Major Changes based on insights

Breaks are based on social worker schedules, which are highly flexible and may not even allow longer breaks. Sometimes workers do not take breaks from work completely.

- We changed our approach from suggesting longer, one hour breaks to shorter, doable break suggestions based on Dialectical Behavioural Therapy.

- Removed linking organisation calendars and left timing of breaks up to them.

There is not much time to reflect during working hours. Workers feel the brunt of the day after they have left the workplace.

- We revamped our day and night mode, limiting our user to functions based on the time of day.

- During the day, users are encouraged to input break types but not spend too much time on the app. After work, users can reflect and evaluate their day.

The app focuses too much on forcing the user to take specific breaks. A more encouraging and affirming approach would be more helpful for social workers.

- We added emphasis to notifications for encouragement but through gentler suggestions and not trying to degrade their day for not taking breaks.

- Changing the wording of the app so that it is encouraging and affirming for the user.

Users did not acknowledge the purpose of the app and why taking breaks are useful for them. Therefore, they may be less convinced to record their breaks.

- We introduced a tutorial where users can understand the purpose of breaks before they start using the app.

- We redesigned the results part, and included smart results where we showed the user how their emotions are largely impacted by the types of break they take.

IV. Iterative Approach

Using feedback heatmaps, we identified pain and delight points to inform prototyping phases.

Due to the limited time we had with social workers, we wanted our product to improve immensely between each iteration so that we can meet the needs of social workers. We found that the best way to justify our design changes to potential stakeholders would be to visualise our changes based on the opinions of experienced social workers. Therefore, we strengthened the connection between previous feedback and our next prototype, giving us confidence that changes we make will result in a better experience for social workers.

V. Quantitative Differences

Our quantitative results show the drastic improvement made to our user interface by listening to participants and designing accordingly.

Timing Participants

For our mid fidelity prototype, we had three tasks for the user to complete. We found that taking a break took the longest due to difficulty finding the add break section and consequently focusing on other parts of the homepage before trying to add a break.

Compared to our mid fidelity testing, it can be seen that our timed results under think aloud protocol has been vastly improved, even with the consideration that some context aware participants had already seen the product before.

Usability Scale

The SUS scale was designed to extract quantitative data. This method is a reliable and valid measure of perceived usability and has become an industry standard with references in over 600 publications. From our testing, we found that out of 5 context-aware participants and 7 general usability participants, we averaged a 62.3 score for our mid-fidelity prototype, which is actually below the scale's standard.

Analysis of our high-fidelity SUS results back up our positive progress in increasing the usability of our product. From the graph below, there is a 21 point difference in the average usability scores compared to the mid fidelity prototype.

After conducting our high fidelity prototype user evaluations, we were able to see how our repeating context-aware participants scored our high fidelity iteration in comparison to our mid fidelity prototype. Hence, we can see if through our iteration process was successful in solving the problem scenario in it's context.

Vi. Outcomes

At A Glance

Social Workers often do not have time to reflect during the day, reflecting after work has finished. The app provides a day mode that encourages the user to take small, manageable breaks.

After Hours

After work, users are able to reflect on their emotional wellbeing and write/vent about their day in dark mode. Emojis are a quick, fun way to record their mood. Featuring a voice recording tool, users can reflect if typing is too much effort.

Connect With Friends

Social workers find that their peers and friends are the best source of comfort. In dark mode, users are given prompts that, if not copied and used as a message, reminds them to contact friends.

Review Previous Reflection

The month view uses different shades of green to summarise each day's emotional rating, conveying to the user how well they are doing. Users can review individual days in more detail, or use the summary on the frontpage to correlate break type and mood.
